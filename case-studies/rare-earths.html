Rare earths and China's dominance

Rare Earths: Abundant Resources, Strategic Dependencies

GeoInsider · May 2025 · Originally published on X / @InsiderGeo

Rare Earths: Abundant Resources, Strategic Dependencies. Despite widespread deposits, China dominates rare earth refining. This thread explores the technical, economic, and geopolitical reasons why: 🧵👇

1/ Rare earth elements (REEs) are 17 metals like:
● Neodymium ● Dysprosium ● Lanthanum.
They power:
● EVs ● Wind turbines ● Smartphones ● Military systems ● Clean tech

2/ Deposits exist in:
● US ● Brazil ● India ● Vietnam ● Australia ● Europe.
The earth's loaded. So what's the catch?

3/ The catch: concentration & separation.
● REEs are scattered in low amounts ● Mixed in ores ● Extracting & purifying requires complex chemistry

4/ Mining REEs is easy. Refining into high-purity forms is hard and that's where most countries fail…

5/ Refining requires:
● Toxic chemicals ● Radioactive waste (thorium) ● Water-intensive processes ● High energy use ● Strict regulations

6/ In the 1990s, China bet big:
● Built refining capacity ● Accepted environmental costs ● Undercut global prices.
By 2010, China controlled over 90% of REE processing.

7/ China doesn't just mine REEs — they mastered the dirty, expensive, technical part: refining.

8/ Example: US's Mountain Pass mine produces REE concentrate — most is shipped to China for final processing…

9/ But why not build refineries at home? Because it's tough:
● $500M–$1.5B+ costs ● Strict regulations ● Political resistance (radioactive waste concerns) ● Needs chemical supply chains & skilled labor

10/ Even with ore, money, and will, building a refinery takes:
● 3–10 years ● Constant government support

11/ China refined the full supply chain:
● Mining ● Refining ● Metal-making ● Magnet production.
They own the entire chain.

12/ Magnets matter. NdFeB magnets power:
● EV motors ● Drones ● Wind turbines ● Missiles.
China makes 90%+ of these magnets.

13/ Mining is just step one. Without refining, countries export raw materials and import high-value products.

14/ REEs matter geopolitically for:
● National security ● Economic sovereignty ● Green energy independence

15/ Countries are reacting:
● US (MP Materials) ● Australia (Lynas) ● EU ● Japan.
Investing in refining capacity, but it's a steep climb, 3–10 years out.

16/ Refineries need:
● Permits & environmental safeguards ● Water, energy, and chemicals ● Skilled workers.
Often multiple refineries per mining region are required.

17/ China's edge isn't just price — it's:
● 30+ years of infrastructure ● Deep know-how ● Political coordination

18/ The US still exports REE concentrates to China:
● Raw materials go out ● High-value products come back.
This creates supply chain risks.

19/ On April 4, 2025, China restricted exports of:
● 7 heavy REEs (samarium, dysprosium, etc.) ● Rare earth magnets.
Exporters need special licenses, halting shipments.

20/ This move was a response to US tariffs and shows why the US is eyeing:
● Ukraine (REE deposits) ● Greenland (vast REE potential).
To cut dependence on China.

21/ That sounds promising but mining REE deposits alone isn't enough. Building refining capacity is the critical, complex step many countries struggle with. Without it, dependence on China's supply chain will persist.

22/ Even if a country has rare earth deposits, without domestic refining, the cost of exporting concentrates abroad creates ongoing dependency.

23/ Shipping ores abroad means paying for transport, refining fees, and facing supply chain risks.

24/ China can impose:
● Export quotas, tariffs, bans.
In 2010, China cut exports to Japan, causing price spikes and supply shocks.

25/ This refining monopoly is a strategic weapon threatening:
● Tech industries ● Defense systems ● Energy supplies.
For countries dependent on China.

26/ The US pushes new mines, but without domestic refining capacity:
● Ukraine's deposits ● Greenland's potential.
Won't break the dependency cycle.

27/ So when you hear about new mining projects, ask:
● Can they refine them? ● Can they make magnets?
That's where real independence starts.

28/ Hope this thread helps many understand the nuances behind rare earths and why dependency on China is so difficult to break.
