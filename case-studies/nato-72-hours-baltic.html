The First 72 Hours: Anatomy of the War NATO Plans to Never Fight

A scenario analysis built from public wargames and defense planning. Not a prediction — a map of where deterrence holds and where it breaks.

GeoInsider · May 20, 2026 · Originally published on Substack

There is a lot of discussion today about the potential for a Russian invasion of the Baltic States. We see drones crossing into Latvia, we have seen a collapse of the government in Riga due to concerns over Russian actions, and we hear the narrative being developed by the Russians at the United Nations. However, virtually all reporting regarding these events ends at the event itself. Virtually none of the reporting addresses the more difficult questions of what happens if an actual conflict develops.

The most commonly referenced answer is more than nine years old. In 2016, RAND completed a simulation of a Russian military operation in the Baltic States with senior U.S. Department of Defense officials and senior representatives of Supreme Headquarters Allied Powers Europe (SACEUR). The results of this simulation defined much of the thinking of Baltic defense planning since then — Russian military forces could reach Tallinn or Riga in 60 to 72 hours. Each of the three Baltic nations has little to no strategic space to lose to buy time. The furthest point in Estonia from the Russian border is approximately 150 miles.

Following is not a prediction, nor is it an unapproved plan. Rather, it is a scenario derived from publicly available wargame simulations and published literature on defense strategy — RAND, the Baltic Defense Initiative, Association of the United States Army (AUSA), and Institut Montaigne — and the public statements made by senior leaders of Baltic and North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) member states. Assume that it represents a conventional attack with hybrid elements — the type of attack the literature believes most likely. Consider it less as a forecast, and more as a map. It illustrates where deterrence succeeds and where it fails.

Why 72 Hours

The number does not represent a random selection. It is determined by the logic of the fait accompli described in Major Robert Rose's essay "The Fait Accompli: Understanding the Logic Behind Russia's Use of Surprise" which appeared in the July 2025 issue of AUSA Landpower Magazine.

Rose describes the Russian reasoning process in detail. Russia would only initiate an offensive if Russia believed it could execute an extremely rapid operation — seize Tallinn, Riga, and Vilnius, create a dense defensive perimeter on the coast, and then gamble that NATO will not be willing to incur significant casualties to retake all of those areas. Therefore, speed is central to the entire Russian calculus. A slow-moving Russian military provides NATO sufficient time to deploy additional reinforcements and converts the battle into precisely the sort of protracted, low-intensity effort Russia seeks to avoid.

As part of their calculations regarding speed, the Russians rely upon conducting Zapad exercises prior to launching an offensive. These are large-scale strategic exercises conducted by Russia every four years. They allow Moscow to assemble forces in concentration without triggering automatic NATO mobilization. Historically analogous to this approach were the maneuvers Egypt engaged in prior to the October 1973 Yom Kippur War. Egyptian President Anwar Sadat concealed his intent to go to war behind a series of apparently innocuous exercises he conducted immediately prior to initiating hostilities.

It is from this history that the 72-hour window derives. NATO's Very High Readiness Joint Task Force (VJTF) — essentially NATO's spearhead — can begin deploying its initial 1,500 personnel within two days, another 3,500 in seven days, and its total reaction force of greater than 300,000 personnel within thirty days. This timeline was established for scenarios that provide clear indications and warning. Should Russia achieve strategic surprise, however, the Baltic nation's military forces and existing NATO battlegroups must hold until the VJTF arrives. All of the events illustrated in the next section depend upon this interval.

Phase 0: Hybrid Crisis (Weeks -4 to -1)

Russia does not initiate wars from a position of strength. The lesson learned from Ukraine is clear — aggression follows months of hybrid activity. In terms of the Baltic region, the weeks preceding any shooting would appear as an escalation of activities that are currently occurring.

Russian cyberattacks against each of the Baltic nation's critical infrastructures — electricity grids, telecommunications networks, railroad systems — would increase in frequency and severity. As previously mentioned, Russian entities continuously probe Baltic networks for vulnerabilities; during just the autumn of 2025 alone there were more than 30 reported hybrid incidents involving NATO and EU countries — including port sabotage and simultaneous railway attacks — per month. A disinformation campaign would target Russian speaking populations primarily located in northern Estonia and Latvia's Latgale region. The purpose of such campaigns would be to create internal tensions and provide Russia with a pretext — i.e., protection of Russian nationals.

Under cover of a Zapad exercise, Russian forces would begin concentrating near the borders with each of the Baltic nations. Existing underwater infrastructure — power transmission lines, oil/gas pipelines, high-speed data lines — would also be sabotaged. For example, as previously noted, the December 2024 attack on the Estlink subsea cable connecting Finland and Estonia demonstrated that this tactic had been employed successfully. Special Operations Teams (Spetsnaz dressed as civilians; GRU Intelligence Officers) would gather information on enemy troop deployments/positions, destroy ammunition caches located forward, disrupt enemy communication networks etc.

To date many of these tactics read like current events rather than hypotheticals. The drone incursions into Latvian airspace, political instability caused by Russian actions, development of narratives by Russia at the UN — these represent Phase 0 activities regardless of whether or not they result in future conflicts.

Phase 1: Initial Attack (Hours 0 – 6)

Russia's opening salvo would involve a high volume fire preparation — known as a Strike Complex — against all three Baltic nations simultaneously. The goal would be to achieve strategic surprise and disrupt/enemy command structure/control.

In order to achieve strategic surprise and create maximum damage, Russia would employ Iskander M short-range ballistic missiles launched from Kaliningrad and Pskov regions targeting:

  • The international airport at Tallinn, Riga and Vilnius
  • The Ämari Air Base
  • The Estonian Defense Forces Headquarters in Tallinn
  • Bases at Tapa, Ādaži and Rukla
  • NATO Fuel Depots
  • Rail Bridges along the Suwalki Corridor

Kalibr Cruise Missiles launched by surface vessels/submarines from the Baltic Fleet would target ports at Klaipeda, Tallinn and Riga thereby preventing NATO naval supply convoys from arriving. Ballistic/Cruise Missile attacks would also disable radar/Tactical Air Defenses systems at locations throughout each country. Simultaneously, a massive cyberattack would disrupt all government/non-government computer networks; power grids; rail systems; and eliminate/harden hardened communications capabilities. Heavy electronic jamming would severely disrupt GPS/tactical communications/artillery guidance systems used by defending forces.

During hours one-three airborne troops would begin moving into position. The 76th Airborne Division — Russia's elite VDV based in Pskov and only forty kilometers from Estonia — would make helicopter assaults on key points including seizing the Narva Bridge and Canal Locks and taking possession of Ämari Air Base. From southern direction special units out of Kaliningrad/Belarus would attempt to sever the Via Baltica Highway and isolate Vilnius.

During hours three-six armored columns from Leningrad Military District would move rapidly along three main axis: in northern Estonia through Narva to Rakvere to Tallinn on E20; in Central Latvia through Daugavpils to Rezekne to Riga via Latgale; and in the decisive southern direction — a two-pronged attack from Kaliningrad in the west and Belarus in the east aimed at cutting the Suwalki Corridor and isolating all three Baltic countries from the remainder of NATO.

Phase 2: Baltic Response (Hours 0 – 72)

Once the initial signs or indications of attack become apparent, each of the Baltic countries' emergency procedures automatically activate. This is fundamentally why all defense planning is structured around this period.

Of the three countries' emergency procedures plans — Estonia's is by far the most advanced. Once activated Estonia's national crisis management system engages; local Kaitseliit units mobilize within two-to-three hours of alert status; Scoutspataljon's CV9035s redeploy from Tapa to predetermined locations; six HIMARS disperse into camouflage hides in wooded areas prepared to fire pre-determined targets with GMLRS or ATACMS rockets; twenty-four K9 Thunder Howitzer systems redeploy into pre-calculated firing positions for counter-battery fires; Estonian Cyber Command initiates protective measures for critical infrastructure.

Lithuania faces perhaps the worst version of this challenge because Suwalki Corridor exists directly between Lithuania proper and Poland. The German 45th Armoured Brigade — consisting of some 2,800 soldiers stationed in Lithuania — rapidly responds as a Rapid Reaction Force. Lithuanian HIMARS units fire the first long-range shots against Russian armored columns. NASAMS-3 Batteries turn on to protect Vilnius. Jointly with Poland, Polish forces — the Polish First Armoured Division and Polish HIMARS — deploy to defend the corridor from the south.

Fundamentally, all defense planning relies upon elastic defense — not fixed boundary defense. The Baltic Defence Line operates as a funnel: anti-tank trenches and pre-positioned obstacles direct enemy armored columns onto limited roads; enemy armored columns encounter anti-tank fire from Javelin/Spike LR2 systems; Territorial Defense Networks — Estonia's Kaitseliit, Latvia's Zemessardze, Lithuania's KASP — conduct urban and forest guerrilla warfare creating costs for enemy occupying every kilometer.

By hours 48 to 72, the battle has reached its turning point. Russia attempts to secure territory gained and establish an outer defensive boundary while NATO begins deploying the VJTF. Elements of the VJTF vanguard will arrive in Poland and begin moving toward the Baltics via the Suwałki Corridor or by sea into Klaipeda, Tallinn, and Riga. The North Atlantic Council will hold an emergency meeting and invoke Article Five.

Phase 3: Counterattacking with NATO (Days 4 – 30)

The overall strategy of this counterattack by NATO follows what Maj. Rose has termed the shield and the sword.

The Shield: the Baltic brigades and NATO's battlegroups will create stable lines of defense for the main avenues of attack, supported by engineering obstacles and providing the necessary depth for stability. The Sword: at least one mechanized brigade per country attacks the penetration points made by Russia in order to prevent those penetrations from developing into an area defense.

NATO achieves fire superiority through the combined use of HIMARS systems used by both the Baltic countries and Poland, prepositioned American ATACMS, and NATO tactical airpower. Between Days 7 and 30, the large follow-up forces of over 300,000 troops arrive — including U.S. V Corps out of Poznań, Great Britain's 3rd Mechanized Division, France and Germany's reserve divisions.

On paper, NATO wins during Phase 3. However, Phase 3 is only successful if the defense was able to survive Phases 1 & 2.

What favors the defense

There are five things that help the defender. First, the terrain. Because there is so much forest and swampy land, and because of all the lakes and rivers in both Estonia and Latvia, the Russians have very few ways to get their tanks anywhere. This limits them to just a couple of routes that a small number of soldiers could protect against. Second, the Russian army will take time to mobilize on the other side of the border, giving defenders time to prepare. Third, long range artillery — HIMARS with ATACMS rockets — allows defenders to hit deep into Russia and hit anything that supports the Russian army. Fourth, the willingness of the population to fight: over 80% of Estonians want to do everything possible to defend their country. Fifth, the deterrent effect of having large allied military units stationed inside the border: Germany in Lithuania, Britain in Estonia, Canada in Latvia. Any invasion automatically means war with three of Europe's largest militaries.

What favors the attack

The initial 72 hours prior to the arrival of the VJTF are solely reliant upon the Baltic forces and the in-place NATO battlegroup. The success of the first 72 hours may be determinative for both sides. The Suwalki Corridor is a major vulnerability — closing it cuts off the Baltic states. Air defenses in the first three days are also a potential liability; the Estonians and Latvians' jointly purchased IRIS-T systems are currently still under development, and Lithuania's NASAMS provides coverage for Vilnius only. Ammunition is a structural liability: the Baltic countries require external supplies for their 155mm and HIMARS rounds, and local munition plants won't begin producing until sometime after 2027 or 2028. Finally, a reduction in U.S. involvement with Europe could potentially create instability, primarily due to the reliance on U.S.-provided ATACMS and U.S. intelligence support for operations across the entire defensive effort.

Where it's decided

This map does not predict that the war will happen; it only shows where the battle will be won or lost if it ever does.

According to all versions of the scenario the Baltics would win or lose the first three days. Not in the Russian initial blitz, which though brutal is survivable. Not in NATO's counterattack, which on paper succeeds. But in between them — the gap where the Baltic forces and the battlegroups already on the ground have to hold alone. During this time Suwałki is either opened or closed. Ammunition lasts or runs out. An ally's political will is tested in real time.

As long as Russia believes the cost of those first three days is too high to be worth it, deterrence works. Everything the Baltics have done since 2022 — the German brigade, HIMARS, the Baltic Defence Line, total-defense societies — exists to keep this belief intact.

The war NATO plans for is the one it intends to never fight. The plan only works if the other side continues to believe it is.
