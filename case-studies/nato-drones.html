NATO drone defense gaps

Over Denmark and Poland: NATO's Drone Defense Reality

GeoInsider · Jun 2025 · Originally published on X / @InsiderGeo

Thread: Over Denmark and Poland: NATO's Drone Defense Reality

1/NATO is often assumed ready to counter any air threat. But small, low-flying drones expose a critical vulnerability across European airspace. Here's why. 🧵👇

2/Detection remains the main limit
NATO radars are designed for aircraft & missiles, not small UAVs.
Tiny radar cross-section (plastic/composite frames)
Slow or hovering flight → weak Doppler returns
Low altitude (150–500m) → blends into terrain, trees, buildings.

3/Special anti-drone radars exist, but only at select locations. Most of Europe remains a detection blind spot.

4/Identification & rules of engagement
Detection ≠ engagement. Drones must be positively identified before any action:
Mistaking a hobby drone or bird for hostile UAV has legal & operational consequences
Sensor fusion (radar + EO/IR + RF) + human verification = delays

5/Civilian airspace & political oversight severely limit kinetic responses in peacetime.
Even in wartime, identifying multiple or autonomous drones is complex & error-prone.

6/Jets are a blunt instrument
Fighter jets are inefficient for small UAVs:
Its weapons designed for fast, high-altitude targets
Deploying jets is costly, consumes fuel, risks collateral damage over populated areas
Not viable for prolonged low-altitude incursions.

7/Gepard & autocannons are scarce:
Gepard (35 mm radar-guided autocannon) is one of few systems capable or effective
Only a handful of NATO members have usable numbers
Some countries have retired them
Legacy guns (ZPU) are manual & short-range → not bad, but u need many.

8/Electronic warfare (EW) is limited:
Jamming/spoofing can neutralize many UAVs without collateral damage, but:
Autonomous drones are immune
Jamming may disrupt legitimate comms, navigation, aviation In peacetime especially
Coverage across NATO is inconsistent → gaps remain

9/Lasers are still immature
Directed-energy weapons show promise, but:
Short ranges (1–5 km)
Degraded by weather (rain, fog, dust)
Cooling, power, mobility challenges
Not yet a practical replacement for kinetic or EW systems.

10/Swarms & resilience are systemic vulnerabilities
Cheap drones can swarm, overwhelming limited point-defense.
Autonomous drones may evade EW
Kinetic engagement quickly unsustainable
Debris from downed drones can still threaten troops, civilians, infrastructure

11/Lessons from Ukraine:
Ukrainian forces gained extensive experience against Russian UAVs & loitering munitions.
NATO could have trained alongside them
Doctrines & tactics not fully adapted → NATO exposed over European airspace

12/Doctrinal mismatch:
NATO is built for high-value air threats, not ubiquitous low-altitude drones
Everything else flows from this — detection gaps, limited assets, and legal constraints are compounded by a doctrine that assumes jets, missiles, and conventional AA are sufficient.

13/So why do I keep repeating for months that NATO is not ready?
Because the problem is systemic: 1)Detection gaps — current radar & sensors cannot reliably see the drones.
2)Limited assets: Gepards, autocannons, and EW systems are scarce, unevenly distributed.
3)Doctrines.

14/Add legal & collateral constraints, immature tech (lasers/EW), and jets/legacy AA inefficiency.
Result: NATO cannot reliably defend against drones, whether in peacetime or wartime.

15/Takeaway:
Yes, we can shoot down drones.
No, current systems, posture, and doctrine are not sufficient.
Until NATO addresses sensor coverage, integrated EW, distributed point-defense, and doctrinal updates, drones remain a critical vulnerability.

16/I wrote this a few minutes ago, but many couldn't access it. So I'm rewriting it here as a thread to make it easier to follow. For those who say "just radar it": radars aren't perfect. They can miss low-flying drones, misidentify small UAVs as birds etc.

17/The bigger picture: detecting drones is only the first step. Identification, engagement rules, deployment of defenses, and updated doctrines are all critical — and NATO still has gaps in all of these areas. The issue is for everyone; also Ukraine still adapts for many things.

At the same time, NATO's current approach isn't efficient: we spend huge sums on complex systems when cheaper, off-the-shelf solutions could cover most gaps if integrated smartly (remember — we had 3 years of timeline, but ok).
