Cambodia vs. Thailand: A Border Dispute Rooted in History

GeoInsider · Jun 27, 2025 · Originally published on X / @InsiderGeo

What's going on between Cambodia and Thailand? Over the past weeks and months, border tensions have flared up again — soldier clashes, closed checkpoints, leaked calls. But to really get it, we need to dig into history first. Let's break it all down. 👇 1/24 🧵

1/ The Khmer Empire (Cambodia) (9th–15th c.) once dominated much of mainland Southeast Asia including parts of today's Thailand. It left behind vast stone temples like Preah Vihear, Ta Muen Thom, and Ta Krabey — monuments of Khmer power, now split by modern borders.

2/ As the Khmer Empire declined, Siam (modern Thailand) rose, absorbing former Khmer lands. By the 18th–19th centuries, large parts of present-day Cambodia were under Siamese influence. These lands remain symbolically and historically sensitive today.

3/ Cambodia became a French protectorate in 1863. France took control of defining its borders — often without input from local rulers. Thailand, then called Siam, remained independent but ceded some territory to France in exchange for colonial recognition. (Blame the baguette!)

4/ French colonial authorities drew the modern boundary using 1907-era maps. These maps placed temples like Preah Vihear inside Cambodia — a decision Thailand disputed then and now. Thailand has long argued the natural watershed line, not the French map.

5/ Preah Vihear sits atop a steep cliff in the Dangrek Mountains — culturally significant and militarily strategic. Cambodia controls the temple, but Thailand claims adjacent land, especially the high ground, based on differing interpretations of the 1907 border.

6/ During WWII, Thailand allied with Japan and annexed western Cambodia — including Siem Reap, Battambang, and Preah Vihear — from 1941 to 1946. After Japan's defeat, Thailand returned the territory under Allied pressure, but nationalist resentment lingered.

7/ In 1962, the ICJ ruled that Preah Vihear belongs to Cambodia. Thailand accepted the ruling but not the border interpretation. The ruling only applied to the temple, not the adjacent land. That gray area has been contested ever since.

8/ Cambodia sees the entire area around Preah Vihear as theirs — temple, access path, and surrounding land. Thailand argues the court never ruled on the exact border, and maintains military positions nearby. Neither side has ever fully stepped back.

9/ Other ancient Khmer temples — Ta Muen Thom and Ta Krabey — also lie near the border, within disputed zones. Cambodia claims them as part of its heritage. Thailand controls them militarily, arguing they lie on its side of the watershed border.

10/ But, tensions spiked again in 2008 when Cambodia applied for UNESCO World Heritage status for Preah Vihear. Thailand protested, and deadly clashes erupted. More border skirmishes followed in 2011, damaging temples and killing troops on both sides.

11/ In 2013, Cambodia returned to the ICJ for clarification. The court reaffirmed Cambodian sovereignty over Preah Vihear and its immediate area — but again stopped short of drawing the full boundary. That left room for continued military standoff.

12/ Since then, both militaries have dug trenches, built posts, and kept troops in the region. While fighting stopped, the border became heavily militarized. Local communities live under constant tension, with occasional gunfire or troop movements.

13/ In Cambodia, the temples represent a stolen heritage and painful legacy of colonial borders. In Thailand, they symbolize sovereignty and resistance to outside interference. That emotional weight is why neither government backs down easily.

14/ The border is also economic. Over 1 million Cambodians work in Thailand. Border towns rely on trade and daily crossings. Tourism, labor, food, and fuel all flow through this region — so when tensions rise, real lives and local economies suffer.

15/ Cambodia and Thailand normalized relations in the 2010s after deadly clashes in 2008–2011. Trade resumed, tourism flourished, and military activity cooled — but tension flared up again in 2025, after a Cambodian soldier was killed in a brief border firefight on May 28.

16/ Since late May 2025, more than 5,000 Cambodian migrant workers have returned daily amid fears of border closures and rising nationalist hostility inside Thailand. Social media has fueled nationalist tensions, with citizens from both countries exchanging insults.

17/ Cambodia took the border dispute to the International Court of Justice, seeking legal resolution. Thailand rejects this internationalization, insisting the matter is sovereign and bilateral.

18/ The situation worsened after a leaked phone call in mid-June between Thai PM Paetongtarn Shinawatra and former Cambodian PM Hun Sen. Paetongtarn called Hun Sen "uncle" and criticized a Thai commander, triggering nationalist outrage and a political crisis in Thailand.

19/ Facing domestic pressure, Paetongtarn took a harder line: closing more border checkpoints and cutting fuel and supplies to cyber fraud centers operating near the border, blending security concerns with political signaling.

20/ In response, both sides ramped up troop deployments. Thailand announced they were prepared for a high-level operation if needed, while Phnom Penh said its forces — and even former PM Hun Sen — were ready to build trenches and repel any Thai incursion.

21/ The Thailand–Cambodia border clashes have never escalated into full-scale war — they've mostly been tense standoffs, brief skirmishes, and political posturing. But as long as the border remains unresolved, this cycle is likely to keep repeating.

22/ Neither Cambodia nor Thailand wants a full-scale war — and frankly, both can't afford one. Thailand has a much larger, better-equipped military and more economic leverage. But even with that edge, a war would be disastrous for both sides, regionally destabilizing.

23/ Hope this helped clear things up. It's not a simple conflict — history, pride, colonial maps, and modern politics all collide here. ASEAN may try to mediate, but right now it's mostly domestic pressure turning into nationalist posturing. Let's hope cooler heads win out. ✌️

Just to clarify: in terms of global impact, this dispute doesn't really move the needle. It's not a major factor for the world economy but it matters for ASEAN and regional dynamics. This is also why very little is known or talked about internationally — still, it matters a lot to those living it.

Adding to this thread: over the past weeks since I first posted, tensions have escalated even further. While both Thailand and Cambodia publicly said they wanted to de-escalate, the reality was quite different. Cambodia banned Thai movies and TV shows, stopped importing Thai fruits and vegetables, and cut some internet and power links to Thailand. Meanwhile, Thailand tightened border controls, shutting almost all crossings except for essential travel. On the ground, landmines were deployed, troops were repositioned, and several soldiers were injured by the landmines, leading to the current serious clashes we're seeing now.
