The Hormuz Truce Has Collapsed Into a War of Enforcement

GeoInsider · Jul 14, 2026 · Intelligence Desk

The United States is dismantling Iran's coastal strike network and restoring a naval blockade. Tehran is attacking commercial shipping and US bases across the Gulf. The fight is no longer about saving the ceasefire. It is about who can impose the rules of passage through Hormuz.

At 16:00 Eastern Time on 14 July, the United States was due to restore its naval blockade of Iranian ports and coastal areas.

By then, Iran had already declared the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed to unauthorised foreign traffic. Tehran's newly created Persian Gulf Strait Authority said passage had become unfeasible because of US military action. The IRGC had attacked commercial vessels that it accused of using routes Iran had not approved.

Both sides were making the same basic claim in different language: ships would move only under rules backed by their military power.

That is the clearest way to understand the fighting between 11 and 14 July.

This is not simply another exchange of American strikes and Iranian retaliation. It is the enforcement phase of a dispute the June agreement never resolved.

Washington is trying to prove that Iran cannot dictate the terms of navigation through an international waterway. Tehran is trying to show that no ship can safely ignore Iranian authority.

Neither side has yet established full control. But both are building rival systems of coercion around the same narrow stretch of water.

The ceasefire postponed the real problem

The June Memorandum of Understanding suspended hostilities for 60 days, reopened the Strait of Hormuz and created space for talks on Iran's nuclear programme, sanctions, frozen funds and wider political normalisation.

It stopped the first phase of the war. It did not settle the argument that caused the next one.

For Washington, reopening Hormuz meant restoring international navigation.

For Tehran, it meant allowing traffic under an interim political arrangement. Iran did not give up the claim that it could regulate passage, designate authorised routes or punish vessels that ignored its instructions.

The difference looked manageable while the truce held.

It became operational once Washington and its partners began using routes that bypassed the Iranian-approved system.

Limited US strikes on 26 and 27 June were the first sign that the arrangement was failing. By early July, attacks on commercial vessels and successive American strike rounds had turned the dispute back into open conflict.

The agreement did not collapse because one side suddenly abandoned a settled understanding. It collapsed because the understanding was never shared.

The GFS Galaxy attack changed the tempo

On 11 July, the IRGC Navy attacked the Cyprus-flagged container ship GFS Galaxy as it attempted to transit on a route Iran considered unauthorised.

The vessel suffered a fire and significant damage to its engine compartment. One member of the civilian crew was reported missing. The IRGC also said it fired a warning shot at a second, unidentified vessel.

The attack mattered for more than the damage to one ship.

Iran was no longer merely threatening to close Hormuz. It was enforcing a claimed system of maritime authority against a commercial vessel.

The American response was immediate and large.

CENTCOM announced another round of strikes against Iranian coastal surveillance radars, missile and drone storage sites, surface-to-air missile launchers and IRGC vessels operating near the strait.

The target set revealed the logic of the US campaign.

Washington was not yet trying to destroy the Iranian state or conduct a broad strategic bombing campaign across the country. It was concentrating on the military chain that allows Iran to contest shipping: detection, targeting, communications, air defence, anti-ship missiles, drones and small naval craft.

The aim was to break Iran's ability to monitor and punish vessels in Hormuz.

Washington is attacking the coastal kill chain

The renewed US campaign had already begun before the GFS Galaxy attack.

On 7 July, CENTCOM said it struck more than 80 targets, with a particular focus on IRGC naval forces and anti-shipping capabilities. The declared target list included more than 60 fast attack craft, coastal radar sites, command-and-control networks, air-defence systems and anti-ship missile positions.

On 8 July, another round hit roughly 90 targets. The scope expanded to include missile and drone depots, naval infrastructure, coastal logistics and transport links.

The 11–12 July strikes were larger again.

CENTCOM said around 140 targets were hit across southern Iran. These included missile and drone sites, ammunition storage, communications infrastructure, surveillance systems, air defences and naval capabilities.

US forces also used one-way attack sea drones against a submarine and ship maintenance facility at Bandar Abbas. According to CENTCOM, this was the first combat use of the platform.

On 13 July, a five-hour mission struck military targets around Bushehr, Chabahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa and Bandar Abbas. CENTCOM described the targets as coastal defence systems, missile and drone sites and maritime capabilities.

By that point, the declared number of US strikes in the renewed campaign had passed 300.

A note on assessment

That figure tells us something important about scale. It does not tell us how much of Iran's capability was actually destroyed.

CENTCOM statements establish what the United States says it targeted. They do not provide an independent battle-damage assessment for every radar, launcher, vessel or depot.

Iran may have lost a significant part of its coastal military network. It may also retain mobile systems, concealed launchers, redundant sensors and enough small platforms to continue threatening traffic. The difference will be visible in what happens at sea, not in the number of targets listed in military communiqués.

Destroying targets is easier than securing Hormuz

The American targeting logic is sound.

Iran needs coastal radars to detect vessels, communications networks to distribute targeting information, anti-ship missiles and drones to attack, air defences to protect those assets and small craft to harass or intercept ships.

Remove enough of that system and Iran's ability to enforce its rules should decline.

But opening a maritime corridor is not the same thing as hitting the forces threatening it.

To keep Hormuz reliably open, the United States needs continuous surveillance, missile and drone defence, mine countermeasures, escorts, rescue capacity, functioning regional bases and the ability to respond quickly to mobile launchers or small boats.

It also needs the commercial shipping industry to believe the passage is safe enough to use. That may be the harder task.

According to maritime data cited in the operational dossier, traffic through the strait fell by roughly 52 percent between 10 and 12 July compared with the previous week.

The decline is strategically important even if the channel is not physically sealed.

Iran does not need to stop every vessel. It only needs to convince enough owners, insurers and crews that the risk is unacceptable.

A waterway can remain technically open while becoming commercially unreliable.

Each attack, warning shot or interception increases insurance costs. Each air-defence alert raises questions about misidentification and falling debris. Each new round of strikes makes captains and companies wonder whether the next transit will take place during another retaliation cycle.

The contest is therefore not just over access. It is over confidence.

Iran answered by widening the battlefield

Tehran cannot match American air and naval power symmetrically.

Its answer has been to expand the number of places where Washington must defend itself.

During the night of 12–13 July, Iran launched missiles and drones towards US facilities and partner states across the region.

  • Bahrain reported attacks against areas hosting American military infrastructure, including the headquarters of the US Fifth Fleet.
  • Kuwait said it intercepted ballistic missiles, a cruise missile and multiple drones. A civilian was injured by falling debris.
  • Jordan reported intercepting missiles directed towards Prince Hassan Air Base.
  • Qatar came under attack around Al Udeid Air Base. Iranian sources claimed that a fighter maintenance centre and command facility had been destroyed. Qatar confirmed incoming fire and reported three injuries, including a child, caused by interception debris. It did not confirm the Iranian damage claims.
  • Oman condemned attacks directed towards facilities on its territory. The IRGC claimed it had destroyed American radar or logistics infrastructure, but neither Washington nor Muscat independently confirmed that level of damage.

Iran also claimed attacks on Patriot batteries, HIMARS systems, ammunition depots and US communications sites across the Gulf. Those claims remain claims.

The verified picture is narrower but still significant: Iran launched a broad regional retaliation, several governments activated air defences, projectiles were intercepted and civilians were injured. Tehran demonstrated reach. It did not demonstrate that every named American system was destroyed.

The objective is political as much as military

Iran's attacks on regional bases serve a clear purpose.

The US campaign against Iran depends on a network of bases, airfields, ports, radars, logistics hubs and political agreements spread across several countries. Tehran is trying to turn that network into a liability.

The message to Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan and Oman is blunt: allowing American forces to operate from your territory may draw Iranian fire onto your territory.

Iran does not need to force these governments into a formal break with Washington. It only needs to raise the political cost of supporting further operations. This is deterrence by horizontal escalation.

Instead of responding only against the platforms directly striking Iran, Tehran distributes pressure across the regional architecture that makes those strikes possible.

The strategy also exposes Iran to serious risks.

Qatar and Oman are not only American partners. They are important diplomatic channels. Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Oman and Pakistan were all involved in contacts with Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi while the attacks were underway.

If Iran repeatedly strikes the countries most capable of mediating, it may reduce its own exit options. Regional pressure can restrain Washington. It can also consolidate a wider coalition against Tehran.

The blockade changes the conflict

The American decision to restore the naval blockade marks a shift from retaliation to sustained economic coercion.

CENTCOM said the blockade would apply to vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports and coastal areas.

During the previous blockade, the United States says more than 140 compliant vessels were redirected, nine non-compliant ships were immobilised or neutralised and more than 50 humanitarian vessels were permitted to pass.

Those figures are official American claims, but they indicate the intended scale of enforcement.

The blockade is not the same thing as closing the Strait of Hormuz.

Its purpose is to restrict Iran's maritime access, isolate its ports and impose direct costs on vessels doing business with the country.

But it also creates a second system of permission around the same maritime space.

Iran says ships must follow routes approved by the Persian Gulf Strait Authority.

The United States says ships connected to Iranian ports will be intercepted or redirected under the blockade. Commercial operators now face two rival enforcement regimes.

The argument is no longer only about freedom of navigation. It is about who has the power to define legitimate navigation.

Trump's description of the United States as the "Guardian of the Hormuz Strait" makes that ambition explicit.

So does his proposed 20 percent charge on cargoes receiving American protection.

The legal and operational details of that tariff remain unclear. But politically, it creates an awkward contradiction.

Washington rejects Iran's right to impose unilateral conditions on passage while considering a system in which commercial ships would pay for American protection.

Iran wants permits. Washington is discussing a security charge. Both are trying to convert military presence into administrative authority.

Three ways to read the escalation

The first explanation is that the United States is conducting a limited coercive campaign. Under this reading, Washington wants to destroy enough of Iran's anti-shipping capability to force Tehran back into the June arrangement or a revised deal. The concentration of strikes on southern Iran and maritime targets supports this interpretation.

The weakness is that Hormuz is not a secondary issue for Tehran. It is Iran's strongest conventional lever over the international economy. Giving up that leverage without major concessions would amount to accepting the strategic terms of defeat.

The second explanation is that Washington is building a new, longer-term security order around the strait. The blockade, the "Guardian" language and the proposed transit charge suggest something broader than a short punitive operation: an American attempt to become the main authority protecting and regulating maritime access.

The weakness is practical. Maintaining that order would require long-term military presence, constant defence of regional bases and the cooperation of governments that are already being targeted.

The third explanation is that Iran is trying to close Hormuz completely. That is possible, but it may overstate Tehran's objective. A total closure would damage Iran's own economy, threaten its partners and increase pressure for a much wider international response. Selective enforcement is more useful. Iran can permit some vessels, threaten others and keep the market uncertain. That preserves Hormuz as leverage without requiring Tehran to stop every ship.

The evidence so far fits a struggle for conditional passage more closely than a clean attempt at total closure.

Best current assessment

The conflict has entered a war of enforcement.

The United States is trying to dismantle Iran's ability to police Hormuz and replace it with an American-backed security regime.

Iran is trying to prove that no security regime can function without Iranian consent.

Neither side currently controls the strait in the full strategic sense.

Washington can strike Iran's coastal military network, escort ships and blockade Iranian ports. It has not yet shown that it can guarantee normal commercial traffic at an acceptable cost.

Tehran can attack vessels, slow maritime movement and threaten the bases supporting US operations. It has not shown that it can close Hormuz completely or enforce its permit system without suffering sustained military damage.

The most likely near-term outcome is therefore not decisive control by either side.

It is a contested maritime regime in which ships continue to move, but under armed protection, changing routes, higher insurance costs and constant political scrutiny.

Every transit becomes a test. A ship that follows an Iranian-approved route strengthens Tehran's claim. A ship that crosses safely under American protection strengthens Washington's. A ship that is hit changes the calculation for everyone behind it.

What to watch next

  • The actual enforcement of the American blockade. Ship diversions, boardings, immobilisations and public instructions to commercial operators will show whether the announcement has become an operational system.
  • The Persian Gulf Strait Authority. If Iran continues issuing selective permissions and attacking vessels that ignore them, Tehran will be trying to turn wartime coercion into a permanent regulatory structure.
  • Commercial traffic. A sustained recovery would suggest the American campaign is restoring confidence. Continued decline would show that Iran can still shape shipping behaviour despite the strikes.
  • Verified damage to US facilities. Iranian claims of destroyed command centres, radar systems, Patriot batteries and logistics infrastructure remain largely unconfirmed. Independent evidence of major damage or American casualties would change the escalation calculus.
  • Diplomacy. Calls involving Pakistan, Oman, Saudi Arabia and Turkey show that channels remain open. But mediation will become harder if those same states or their neighbours continue to come under attack.
