K-SWARM: Leonardo and Baykar's Latest Trial Deserves Attention — But Not Because Europe Suddenly Has a Combat-Ready "Loyal Wingman"

GeoInsider · Jun 22, 2026 · Originally published on X / @InsiderGeo

During the test campaign at Baykar's Çorlu facility, a Leonardo M-346FA flew alongside the KIZILELMA unmanned combat aircraft. KIZILELMA taxied, took off and joined the formation autonomously, while the M-346 crew commanded changes in position, separation and rejoin manoeuvres through a newly integrated avionics and data-link architecture.

Two independently developed Italian and Turkish platforms were able to communicate and operate together in live flight, moving the program beyond simulations and corporate presentations.

Flying in formation under controlled conditions is very different from operating inside contested airspace.

There is no public evidence that the system has yet been tested against heavy electronic warfare, degraded communications or modern air defences. Nor have we seen weapons employment, autonomous targeting or one crew managing several unmanned aircraft during a complex combat mission.

In other words, this is an important building block — not yet an operational loyal-wingman capability.

The industrial side may actually be the most interesting part of the story.

Baykar brings rapid development, relatively affordable unmanned platforms and an increasingly large export network. Leonardo brings sensors, mission systems, secure communications, certification experience and access to European and NATO procurement channels.

Put together, the two companies could offer European customers a practical crewed-uncrewed solution well before the continent's more ambitious next-generation combat-air programs are ready.

The political conditions imposed by Rome also matter. Italy appears determined to keep control over where the technology can be exported and how it can be developed, particularly outside the European and NATO sphere. That suggests the partnership is being treated as a strategic capability, not simply another commercial drone project.

Europe may be trying to close part of its unmanned combat-air gap through cooperation with Türkiye, combining Turkish speed and production capacity with Italian systems integration and certification.

K-SWARM is still at an early stage. But the Leonardo–Baykar partnership has now moved from agreements on paper to integrated aircraft in the air — and that makes it worth watching closely.
