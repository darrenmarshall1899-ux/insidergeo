How China Is Actually Helping Iran in This War

GeoInsider · Apr 2025 · Originally published on X / @InsiderGeo

Thread: How China Is Actually Helping Iran in This War 1/ On April 19, US Navy forces intercepted the Iranian container ship MV Touska in the Gulf of Oman. It was returning from China, carrying dual-use equipment. That seizure is the most concrete recent example of what Chinese support to Iran actually looks like in this war, a network of civilian and commercial channels, assembled over a decade, carrying things with obvious military applications. This thread walks through how those channels function. 🧵⬇️

2/ Start with navigation. Iran got military-level access to BeiDou in 2021, confirmed by the US DoD's own China Military Power Report. Military BeiDou delivers sub-meter precision and carries a short-message service for command continuity. The key advantage from Iran's perspective is that BeiDou sits outside US sovereign control. Washington can jam Iranian receivers, electronic warfare works on any GNSS, but it cannot selectively degrade the system against Iran, deny code access, or turn it off. With GPS, Washington can do all three. ⬇️

3/ The practical consequence is that most newer Iranian precision-guided systems, missiles, loitering munitions, command nodes, are now running Chinese navigation. In March, the US-China Economic and Security Review Commission publicly stated that BeiDou was being used by Iran to direct attacks across the region. That's a US government assessment, not a private analyst's read. The improved missile accuracy since the 12-day war of June 2025 is consistent with this migration, though multiple variables make the precision-gain attribution imperfect. ⬇️

4/ Above navigation sits the satellite layer. In April the Financial Times reported, based on leaked Iranian military documents, that the IRGC Aerospace Force acquired a Chinese commercial imaging satellite in late 2024 — TEE-01B from Earth Eye Co., operated through ground stations run by Emposat. Roughly half-meter panchromatic resolution, a serious jump over the Noor-2/3 series Iran had been running. Caveat worth stating: this is FT's investigation, and the provenance of the underlying documents hasn't been independently verified. The Chinese Ministry of Foreign Affairs has denied the account. ⬇️

5/ The AI layer sits above that. ABC reported, citing DIA sources, that MizarVision — a Hangzhou AI firm with small state equity — has been publishing satellite imagery tagged with military-capability labels: aircraft types at US bases, ship positions, air defense deployments. Jing'an Technology, another Chinese firm with PLA links, markets the same kind of product. US officials say Iran uses these feeds for targeting. What's independently verifiable is that the feeds exist, circulate on Chinese and Western social media, and are commercially sold, though Iran's sanctioned status means any direct sale to Tehran is itself sanctions exposure. ⬇️

6/ The pattern running through the satellite layer is structural to the whole architecture. The companies involved are privately held and commercially motivated. The imagery is openly sold. Iran pays alongside other customers. Beijing can honestly say it isn't providing military ISR because formally it isn't. Ahead of a planned Xi-Trump summit in May, Xi personally wrote to Trump to deny any Chinese weapons supply to Iran — the kind of explicit head-of-state denial the whole architecture is built to make possible.

7/ Beneath the ISR infrastructure runs a quieter trade in dual-use chemistry. CNN reported, citing Western intelligence, that Iran received roughly 2,000 tons of sodium perchlorate from Chinese suppliers during 2025 — a precursor for solid-fuel missile propellant. Intelligence estimates converting that tonnage into missile-equivalents vary with class and payload, but put the order of magnitude in the low hundreds. The US Treasury sanctioned multiple Iranian vessels in the shipping network earlier this year. The MV Touska seizure on April 19 is the most recent interdiction in the same chain. ⬇️

8/ INSS traced part of the academic backstory to the chemistry flow. Researchers at Beijing Institute of Technology and the University of Mazandaran co-authored work in 2023-24 on ammonium perchlorate decomposition — mapping the thermal path at atomic scale, then showing how specific catalysts improve propellant burn performance. It's research a civilian propulsion lab would also produce, which is how the channel does its work.

9/ That academic channel is bigger than it sounds. The INSS study covers 753 co-authored papers between China's seven defense-linked universities (the "Seven Sons" — HIT, NUAA, NWPU, BUAA, BIT, NJUST, HEU) and Iranian institutions between 2006 and 2025. Fifteen have direct military applications: submarine acoustic signature reduction, UAV composites, reactor cooling models, missile propellant chemistry. Small as a proportion, steady across nearly two decades, and outside the reach of standard export controls entirely.

10/ Putting the channels together: navigation (BeiDou), imagery (commercial satellites plus AI tagging), propellant chemistry, and a sustained applied-research pipeline through defense universities. Beijing classifies every one of these as civilian or commercial. Washington disputes the classification on several — SMIC's chipmaking tool transfers to the Iranian military and the sodium perchlorate supply chain have both been US-sanctioned as militarily relevant. But on Beijing's ledger, nothing crossing the arms-transfer threshold is in play. That gap between the two classifications is where the entire architecture lives.

11/ Worth naming what Beijing has kept off the table: no J-10C fighter transfers, no Chinese warships escorting Iranian shipping in the Gulf, no direct combat role. CNN reported on April 11 that China was preparing MANPADS shipments via third countries, but that's the preparation stage — Xi denied it, and no delivery has been confirmed. At the same time, Beijing has moved heavily on the diplomatic side: Wang Yi made 26 calls between February 28 and the April 8 ceasefire, Xi personally nudged Khamenei toward agreement, and the China-Pakistan 5-point initiative of March 31 shaped the framework the Islamabad talks used. At the Security Council, Beijing is active, not passive — on April 7, China and Russia vetoed a Bahrain-sponsored draft on Hormuz shipping escorts.

12/ The strong version of "China is helping Iran in this war" isn't that Beijing is running an overt arms pipeline. The evidence for direct weapons transfer remains thin, Xi has it on the record denying it, and the MANPADS reporting is at the preparation stage rather than confirmed delivery. What's actually happening is that Iran is fighting a 2026 war against the United States using Chinese navigation, Chinese imagery, Chinese precursor chemistry, and research pipelines rooted in Chinese defense universities — while Beijing, by its own classification, provides no military aid. That architecture took over a decade of civilian-commercial channeling to assemble. Four months into open conflict, it's still intact, and that durability is a stronger signal about Chinese strategy than any single shipment would be.
