The Russia-China-Iran Triangle

What 74 Days of the Iran War Tell Us About the Next Phase

GeoInsider · May 12, 2026 · Originally published on Substack

Trump leaves for Beijing tonight. Iran rejected the US peace memo two days ago, called it a "garbage" response when Trump read it. The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff told the Senate this afternoon that the Pentagon's primary focus is now "deterrence vs Xi Jinping." And Tehran has been signalling for four days that the Trump-Xi summit shouldn't happen on Washington's terms.

These four things happened in 72 hours. Every news outlet covered them separately. Read together they say something different.

This is what GeoInsider is for. Putting back together what mainstream coverage takes apart.

The pattern was already there

The triangle didn't start with the Iran war. It started in January 2025, when Putin and Pezeshkian signed the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership Treaty in Moscow. 20 years, automatic renewals. Defense, intelligence, energy, cyber, anti-sanctions finance. First major Russia-Iran agreement since 2001.

China was already there. Xi and Putin had reinforced their own partnership at the Moscow summit a few months earlier. The phrase that started circulating in policy circles was "axis of upheaval" — Russia, China, Iran, North Korea, all aligned against the US-led order. The phrase oversells. There's no formal alliance. There's no mutual defense clause. Russia and China don't agree on much beyond opposing US hegemony.

But the convergence is real, and the 2026 Iran war has been the stress test. What I'm going to show you is that all three players got what they wanted out of it, in different ways and for different reasons. None of them are paying the costs the war is supposed to impose on them. That's the structural fact most coverage misses.

What Russia got: rent without risk

Russia's gains from this war are documented and quantifiable.

Start with the windfall. The Iran-US war pushed Urals crude from $44 a barrel to $77-115. Russian oil revenue went from $4.5B to $9B a month. In March 2026, Russia was making €713M a day in fossil fuel revenue, €7.4B a month in fiscal revenue. Chatham House called it "an economic gift to Putin." Foreign Policy summed it up: "Russia is winning the war in Iran without fighting it."

The paradox sharpens when you look at the US response. In March, the House passed a waiver on Russian oil sanctions to calm the global prices that the Iran war had blown up. The G7 $46 price cap went out the window. Trump indirectly bankrolls the Russian economy to manage a crisis his administration helped create. One of the biggest policy paradoxes of the decade.

On the military side, Russian support to Iran is real but calibrated. The Wall Street Journal reported on March 17 that Russia is feeding Iran imagery from its VKS satellite constellation, with granular data on US troop movements. A Ukrainian intelligence assessment via Reuters documented 24 Russian reconnaissance passes across 11 Middle Eastern countries between March 21 and 31, covering 46 locations. Reuters couldn't verify the assessment independently, but Western sources confirmed Russian satellite activity in the region. Right after those passes, Iranian missiles and drones hit some of those same targets.

There's more. The Shahed drone cycle goes both ways. Iran shipped 600 disassembled Shahed-136s to Russia plus components for another 1,350 under a $1.75B contract signed in 2023. By 2025, about 90% of assembly had moved to Alabuga in Tatarstan. Production hits roughly 5,000 a month according to ISW, though Ukrainian intelligence specifies that about half are unarmed decoy drones. Russia upgraded the design and integrated the Kometa-M satellite navigation receiver, jam-resistant, onto Iranian drones. Le Monde documented that identical tactics — waves of decoys followed by precision strikes — are now used by Iran against Gulf countries. The expertise refined in Ukraine going back to Tehran via Crimea training sessions.

S-400 deliveries: OSINT tracked 7-8 An-124 and Il-76 flights from Russian military facilities to Iran between October and November 2025, coinciding with Iranian ballistic tests. Analyst inference, not confirmed delivery. Su-35 jets: UNITED24 leaked documents confirm 16 Flanker-Es in active production for Iran, with options up to 50 by 2027.

Here's what Russia didn't do. No mutual defense clause in the January 2025 treaty. When Israel struck Iran in June 2025, Moscow watched. Carnegie and CSIS both spell it out: Russia is not obligated to defend Iran if Iran gets attacked, and chose not to. Intentional.

The pattern is clear. Russia gives Iran enough to keep it in the strategic orbit, denies everything publicly to preserve the Washington channel on Ukraine, and pockets $9B a month from the oil prices the war produces. Putin is the rentier, not the patron.

What China got: discount and time

China's gains are quieter, and that's the point.

Beijing is the largest beneficiary of sanctioned Iranian oil. Treasury designated Qingdao Haiye Oil Terminal Co. last week for importing "tens of millions of barrels" of Iranian crude. The company's president was sanctioned. A dark fleet tanker, the New Fusion, and its operating company in the Marshall Islands were also designated. The Chinese government rejected the designations as "unilateral sanctions" — and continued buying.

China gets Iranian oil at a steep discount because Tehran has nowhere else to sell. Estimates put the discount somewhere between 20-30% off market price. While the rest of the world pays Brent above $100, Chinese refiners get cheap crude that wouldn't exist as a market without the war. The blockade isn't a problem for China. It's a structural advantage.

Then there's the industrial pipeline. ISW reporting based on US sources documents Chinese components feeding the Iranian missile industry — guidance systems, propellants, raw materials. None of this is new. What's new is the scale during the war: the deeper Iran's stockpile gets drained by the conflict, the more dependent it becomes on Chinese resupply.

Diplomatically, China plays the long game. Wang Yi met Araghchi in Beijing last week — Iran's first foreign minister visit to China since the war began. The public message was "comprehensive ceasefire, resumption of hostilities not acceptable." Translation: China wants the war to end on terms that don't break Iran completely. Beijing needs Tehran functional as a counterweight and as an oil supplier. Iran needs Beijing as a buyer and as a diplomatic shield. The interests align.

The asymmetry with Russia matters. Russia gives Iran military capability and intelligence. China gives Iran finance, market access, and industrial inputs. Russia takes the strategic risks of weapons transfers. China takes the commercial benefits without the headlines. If you had to bet on which side of the triangle is more durable in the next five years, you'd bet on China.

And here's the kicker: China doesn't want this war to end too fast. Discounted oil, distracted US, US-Gulf alliances under stress — these are the conditions Beijing has been preparing for. The longer the war drags on with no clean resolution, the better positioned China is for its real strategic objective, which isn't the Middle East. It's Taiwan.

This is why Gen. Caine's statement to the Senate this afternoon matters more than people are reading it. The Pentagon's primary focus is now Xi Jinping deterrence. The Iran war isn't the strategic priority anymore. It's the distraction that makes the strategic priority harder.

Iran as the shared platform

Iran isn't the recipient of the triangle. It's the infrastructure of it.

Geographically, Iran is the only piece of the puzzle that connects Russia to the Indian Ocean and China to the Persian Gulf without going through US-aligned territory. The INSTC corridor — the International North-South Transport Corridor — runs from Russia through Iran to India over 7,200 km, transit times 40% shorter than the Suez Canal. February 2025 saw the agreement to close the Rasht-Astara missing link. November 2025 saw the Makhachkala port consortium with a target of 5 million tons a year. For Moscow it's a lifeline around the Baltic and Black Sea. For Beijing, Iran is the western anchor of the Belt and Road minus US chokepoints.

Operationally, Iran provides what Russia and China can't risk providing directly. The Houthis disrupt Red Sea shipping under Iranian sponsorship, raising costs for European trade without Russian or Chinese fingerprints. Hezbollah keeps Israel pinned down, draining Israeli and US resources. The IRGC closes the Strait of Hormuz, hitting Gulf states that are US partners. Every one of these moves serves the larger triangle without requiring Moscow or Beijing to act openly.

Tehran knows it's being used. The strategic asymmetry is obvious — Russia gets $9B a month, China gets discounted oil, Iran gets sanctioned, blockaded, and bombed. But the regime has no alternatives. Survival of the Islamic Republic depends on having backers, and the only backers that don't demand regime change are Moscow and Beijing. So Tehran trades strategic margin for operational support, and the triangle holds.

What does Iran actually gain? Less than it gives. But the alternative is isolation, and isolation against US-Israel means collapse. From Tehran's perspective, asymmetric partnership beats no partnership.

Why Trump's Beijing trip matters now

Read everything I've written so far, then look at the calendar.

Sunday: an advisor to Mojtaba Khamenei warns Trump via Al Jazeera not to "visit China as a victor, taking advantage of our calm today." Tuesday morning: Rezaei from the Iranian parliament threatens 90% enrichment if Iran is attacked again — that's weapons-grade, the line Tehran has never publicly crossed. Tuesday afternoon: Caine tells the Senate the Pentagon's primary focus is China. Tuesday night: Trump flies to Beijing with the Iran deal stuck.

Tehran spent four days trying to deter the trip, then trying to raise the cost of it. None of it worked. Trump goes anyway. That tells you something: Washington has recognized that the bilateral Iran-US negotiation isn't working, and the only way forward is through Beijing or Moscow. The triangle is now part of the negotiation, not just the strategic backdrop.

Two scenarios to watch in the next 72 hours.

One, Trump uses Beijing to push Xi to pressure Tehran. Carrot-and-stick combo: trade concessions for Iran cooperation. China gets the role of broker and the prestige of being the one who ends the war. The cost to Washington is acknowledging that the unipolar moment is over and that you need Beijing to close Middle Eastern deals.

Two, Xi stays neutral or pro-Iran. Signals that the triangle is functional. Buys more discounted oil. Lets the US-Iran stalemate continue because every month of stalemate is a month US strategic capacity is tied up in the Gulf instead of the Pacific.

The second scenario is what Beijing actually wants. The first is what Washington is hoping for. The summit will tell us which one is coming.
