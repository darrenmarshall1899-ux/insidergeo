The Space Force Is Fighting Iran. There's Nothing to Show for It.

What Kept the Gulf Bases Standing: Missile Warning in the War With Iran.

GeoInsider · Jun 1, 2026 · Originally published on Substack

Most of the war with Iran that reached the public was the part you could film: B-2s lifting off, smoke over Jebel Ali, carrier decks, the bunker-buster footage from last June. The thing that did the most to decide whether the troops in Qatar or Kuwait woke up the next morning never made any of it. It runs in geosynchronous orbit, and on a console at Al Udeid, and there is nothing there to point a camera at.

That thing is missile warning, and it is worth taking on its own, not as a footnote to the strike packages.

The physics sets the whole problem. A ballistic missile fired from Iranian soil reaches a Gulf base in a few minutes. Put that next to a launch from China toward Guam and it becomes obvious why the people who run this mission keep going back to time of flight. There is almost no margin in it. A warning that comes late, or comes wrong, is the same as no warning.

The system behind it is old, which is the part outsiders tend to miss. It goes back to the Defense Support Program satellites the United States started putting up in the early 1970s, built to catch the infrared flare of a Soviet ICBM the second its booster lit. The Space-Based Infrared System, SBIRS, took over from those across the last decade and a half, and the next set, Next-Generation OPIR, is being built now. The job has stayed the same the whole time: see the plume from orbit within seconds and get the track down to someone who can act on it. What changed is whose launches they are. Hardware built to watch for a Soviet first strike now spends a lot of its time over Iranian transporter-erector-launchers.

Detection is only the first link. The infrared track goes to radars on land and at sea. They tighten the trajectory and work out where it will come down, then hand the cue to the interceptors: Patriot, THAAD, the Aegis ships sitting offshore. None of those fire without that first call from orbit.

That clock is why, in 2022, U.S. Central Command stood up its own Space Force component, Space Forces Central. It started small, a few dozen Guardians forward at Al Udeid, and the reason given at the time was exactly this short-flight-time problem. Somebody had to tie the space-based warning to the missile defense that was actually sitting in the theater. When the Chief of Space Operations, Gen. Chance Saltzman, said this spring that his people were deployed "inside the threat zone," the forward posture is what he meant, literally.

So what did it buy in the war that opened in February? In early March the chairman of the Joint Chiefs, Gen. Dan Caine, said the integrated air-and-missile-defense network was doing what it was built to do, and that together those systems had knocked down hundreds of Iranian ballistic missiles aimed at American forces and their partners. At one point batteries were firing out of Qatar, the UAE, Kuwait, Jordan and Saudi Arabia at the same time. That is real, and it would be dishonest to play it down.

It also leaked. Hundreds stopped is not all of them stopped, and Americans died in this war anyway. Caine himself pointed out that the one-way attack drones — slower, lower, far cheaper than the ballistic missiles — stayed a problem from the first week to the last. The architecture is tuned against fast ballistic threats. It was never a roof over the whole theater, and Iranian planners knew that, which is most of why the drones kept coming.

This is the point where the honest version has to stop, even though the dramatic one would keep going. The commander of U.S. Space Command, Gen. Stephen Whiting, has called American missile warning genuinely one of a kind, able to catch launches anywhere on earth and pass them in real time to troops in the field and to the President. That is the on-record claim, and it is probably close to true. What you do not get is which satellites, tasked how, with what coverage over which part of Iran. That part is classified. The rest of what circulates — the jamming, the "blinding" of Iranian sensors, the targeting help — comes either from senior officers being deliberately vague or from outside analysts using the word "likely" a great deal. It is reasonable to think space did a lot here. What it actually looked like is something we do not know, and that gap is worth keeping in view.

There is a tail to this worth noticing. If the warning layer is what keeps the bases standing, it is also the thing most worth taking out, which is why the same officials who will not discuss their satellites keep reaching for the phrase "space superiority." Nobody pays attention to the layer until someone tries to take it away.

The footage from this war will be jets and carriers, because that is what there is to put on a screen. The part that did the most to decide who got through a given night is more than twenty thousand miles up. It is older than most of the Guardians running it now. And there is no footage of it at all.
